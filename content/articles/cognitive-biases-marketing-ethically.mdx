Cognitive biases are not shortcuts for tricking people. They are clues about how people process complexity under time pressure, limited attention and incomplete information.

The ethical opportunity is to make good decisions easier.

## Biases reveal friction

If customers rely on social proof, they may be uncertain about quality. If they respond to anchoring, they may need a better reference point. If they avoid loss, they may need confidence that the decision is reversible or low risk.

Biases often point to the part of the journey that needs more clarity.

## Use behavior science as a design discipline

Good marketing psychology starts with diagnosis. What is the customer trying to understand? What risk are they managing? What belief has to change? From there, bias-informed design can improve framing, sequencing, proof and choice architecture.

The craft is in removing confusion without removing agency.

## The ethical test

Ask whether the tactic helps a well-fit customer make a decision they are likely to feel good about later. If the answer depends on hiding information, manufacturing panic or exploiting vulnerability, the performance gain is borrowing against trust.

Trust is also a growth asset.
