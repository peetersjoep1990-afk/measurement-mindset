ROAS is seductive because it makes marketing feel accountable. Spend one euro, get several back. Clean ratio, clean story. But the metric becomes dangerous when it is treated as the primary definition of growth.

High ROAS can be a sign of efficiency. It can also be a sign that you are harvesting demand you already had.

## Efficiency can hide shrinking ambition

When teams optimize only for ROAS, budgets often move toward retargeting, branded search and the safest audiences. Those channels convert well because they are close to existing intent. They rarely create enough new demand to expand the future customer base.

The brand looks efficient while the growth engine gets smaller.

## Short-term wins can tax future demand

Performance pressure often pushes creative toward direct-response certainty: discounts, urgency and narrow product claims. Those tactics can work, but they can also train customers to wait, compare and ignore anything that is not immediately promotional.

Growth needs a better balance between capture and creation.

## What to measure instead

Keep ROAS, but demote it. Pair it with incrementality, new customer quality, payback period, contribution margin and brand demand signals. The question is not whether marketing returned money this week. The question is whether it is building a larger and more profitable market over time.
