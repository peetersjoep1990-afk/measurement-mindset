Attribution models are useful until they become a substitute for judgment. They compress a messy reality into a neat distribution of credit, then invite the team to optimize as if the model has seen the whole customer journey.

The problem is not that attribution is always wrong. The problem is that it is often confidently incomplete.

## The model only sees what it can tag

Most attribution systems are strongest where tracking is easiest: click-heavy channels, logged-in environments and bottom-funnel interactions. That means they tend to reward the activity closest to conversion and discount the work that created demand earlier.

This creates a measurement bias that looks like performance insight.

## Precision is not the same as truth

When a dashboard assigns 37.4 percent of revenue to one campaign, the decimal places can make the answer feel scientific. But the number still depends on assumptions about lookback windows, identity resolution, channel interaction and customer intent.

Treat attribution as a lens, not a verdict.

## A better operating model

Use attribution for tactical diagnosis. Use incrementality tests to understand causality. Use marketing mix modeling to evaluate broader channel contribution. Then bring the methods together in a measurement system that makes uncertainty visible.

The strongest teams do not look for one perfect model. They build a habit of triangulation.
